Welcoming the New Team: Meritocracy at JOM

Brian L. Connelly https://orcid.org/0000-0003-1804-8654 bconnelly@auburn.eduView all authors and affiliations
Volume 47, Issue 1
https://doi.org/10.1177/0149206320963191

You cannot buy tickets to an Auburn University basketball game. The team has become so popular that tickets are unavailable. Here in the Management Department at Auburn Universityâs Harbert College of Business, we are growing quickly, owing in large part to having become a named business school. We have hired six newly minted PhDs, an Eminent Scholar, and a new department chair. They are a talented and highly collegial group of scholars; we enjoy each otherâs company and gather together regularly, but never at Auburn University basketball games because none of them can get a ticket. Years ago, entrance was inexpensive, and games were sparsely attended. Then the team got better and better, and suddenly everyone wanted in.
Something similar has happened at the Journal of Management (JOM). A group of scholars in the southeastern United States founded the journal in 1975. In Volume 1, Issue 1, there is an article by four now-retired Auburn University professors. It was five pages long, moved straight from Introduction to Method, and consisted of six t tests. No offense to these four scholars, all of whom went on to have stellar careers, but it was a humble origin for the journal. Then something happened: The journal got better and better. Articles such as Barneyâs work on resources and Podsakoff and Organâs work on self-reports brought substantial visibility. Scholars such as Ricky Griffin at Texas A&M and Dan Dalton at Indiana University brought growing levels of prestige. With each passing editorial team, the journal grew in stature, and as a result, entrance into the pages of the journal become increasingly difficult. Today, everybody wants in.
For the Auburn University basketball team, there was one primary means by which it attained its enviable position: recruiting great players. Like many other basketball programs, Auburn did a lot of things right: It built a nice stadium, it hired knowledgeable coaches, and the players worked hard to field a competitive team. Everything changed, though, when Auburn began to have some recruiting success. The factor that most accounted for Auburnâs rise to national prominence on the court was that it had world-class basketball players.
There may be a sense in which this is also true of JOMâs rise to prominence. Like many other journals, we have done a lot of things right through the years. We provided fast decisions, maintained scientific integrity, and worked hard to build a competitive scientific journal. What most set us apart, though, is that we have been able to recruit some of the best scholars in management to lead the journal. In addition to those mentioned already, we have seen such talented and respected editors as Tayla Bauer, Deb Rupp, Pat Wright, and David Allen, among others. It is with humility that I take my place in this line of esteemed leaders.
There are other players on the JOM team that are also essential to our success, and perhaps most important of these is the editorial team that handles papers submitted to the journal. My first task as editor in chief was to recruit the best players for this foundational position. Some have said that JOM is a meritocracy. There is no higher accolade, and the new editorial team reflects this well-established culture of the journal. Specifically, I carefully examined top management journals over the past two decades and systemically evaluated and ranked management scholars by productivity. Other key decision-making factors, such as the trajectory of their scholarly career, reputation as reviewers, and diversity of their scholarship, were also important. The resulting group of associate editors is the best in the world at what they do. They, on average, have 16 top-tier management publications, they have been cited over 4,000 times, and each has published work in JOM. They have earned the moniker The Next Generation of Thought Leaders in the Academy.
The editorial team begins with three senior associate editors. If you submit a paper to JOM, I will read it and conduct an initial assessment of its suitability for the journal. If I do not desk-reject the paper, I will send it to one of these three people. Micro papers go to Chad Van Iddekinge (University of Iowa), who studies how organizations make staffing decisions and how those decisions affect job applicants and the quality and diversity of a firmâs workforce. Macro and entrepreneurship papers go to Zeki Simsek (Clemson University), whose own research resides at the intersection of strategic leadership, innovation, and entrepreneurship. Methods papers are handled by Kris Byron (Georgia State University), whose research interests include perception, creativity, diversity, and relationships and who is in the officer track of the Research Methods Division of the Academy of Management. These three bring a wealth of editorial experience, having served, respectively, as associate editors at Personnel Psychology, Academy of Management Journal, and Academy of Management Review.
On the micro side, we have seven associate editors. Chia-Huei Wu (University of Leeds) has research interests that include proactivity at work, personality development, and subjective well-being. Pauline Schilpzand (Oregon State University) does work on counterproductive workplace behavior, proactive performance, and diversity. Chris Rosen (University of Arkansas) does research on workplace politics, self-regulation, and employee well-being. Ning Li (University of Iowa) does research on teams, organizational network analysis, creativity, and big data. Jason Huang (Michigan State University) covers research on human resources, adaptability at work, training, and skills transfer. Rebecca Greenbaum (Rutgers University) conducts research on behavioral ethics, dysfunctional leadership, and workplace deviance. Mark Bolino (University of Oklahoma) studies organizational citizenship behavior, impression management, and global careers. We are especially thankful to Chris and Rebecca, both of whom were appointed to the prior editorial team and agreed to carry over, providing the journal with a measure of continuity.
For macro and entrepreneurship papers, we have eight new associate editors. Tieying Yu (Boston College) conducts work on competitive dynamics, corporate communication, and global strategy. Toyah Miller (University of TexasâDallas) does research on institutions and internationalization, social entrepreneurship, and diversity. Sali Li (University of South Carolina) is focused on multinational strategy, digital platforms, international entrepreneurship, and the demand-side perspective. Ryan Krause (Texas Christian University) does work on boards of directors, top management teams, and stakeholder management. Aaron Hill (University of Florida) conducts research on corporate governance, strategic leadership, diversity, and corporate social responsibility. Pursey Heugens (Erasmus University) brings expertise on the strategy and governance of organizational forms, such as family firms, state-owned enterprises, business groups, professional service firms, and entrepreneurial ventures. Gokhan Ertug (Singapore Management University) does research on status, reputation, social networks, knowledge management, inequality, and international business. Last, Jim Combs (University of Central Florida) is revitalizing the journalâs emphasis on entrepreneurship and brings special expertise in the areas of franchising and family business.
I have also added a new role to the editorial team in the form of consulting editors, who operate as mentors and advisors for me. Rob Ployhart (University of South Carolina) and Dave Ketchen (Auburn University) serve in this capacity and have already proven to be invaluable additions to the journal. Together, they have editorial experience at the Academy of Management Journal, Academy of Management Review, Academy of Management Perspectives, Journal of Applied Psychology, Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, Journal of International Business Studies, Journal of Operations Management, Journal of Supply Chain Management, Decision Sciences Journal, Organizational Research Methods, and Business Horizons. Given their wealth of experience, these two individuals constitute a steadying influence for the journal and will provide unique insight and wise counsel as we move forward with new people, processes, and ideas.
I also want to thank and acknowledge the key players who brought JOM to the place it is today. David Allenâs editorial team did stellar work and raised the journal to new heights. His senior associate editors were Eden King, Fred Oswald, and Taco Reus, and the Methods Task Force chair was Don Bergh. Associate editors included Bianca Beersma, Gerard Hodgkinson, Brian Hoffman, Luis Martins, Ernest OâBoyle, Anne Parmigiani, Christopher Porter, Karen Schnatterly, Bill Schulze, Jorge Walter, and William Wan. We owe them a debt of gratitude for their service to the journal and the profession.
It might be fair to say there are three types of players on the JOM team: the leaders, the supporters, and the haymakers. The editors described previously are the leaders, and I am thrilled about the star-studded group we have assembled for that role. Our reviewers are the supporters as they serve behind the scenes. This group is led by the Editorial Review Board (ERB). I am pleased to say that nearly one third of our ERB team is new, and they bring considerable energy and enthusiasm to this critical role. We sought to infuse this portion of our team with emerging scholars that have established themselves in the top management journals in recent years and are working at the nexus of emerging ideas. Last, the haymakers are our authors. For JOM to continue to rise in stature and prominence, we need you to submit your best work. What we can promise in return is a fast turnaround, fair decisions, and constructive advice from a highly qualified team of experts. Our goal is to stay true to the meritocratic culture that brought us here.
You are going to see some big things from JOM in 2021. For example, you are going to see editorial commentaries on such pressing topics as responding to COVID-19, grasping the extent to which systemic racism pervades workplaces and our academic profession, and understanding the consequences of sexual harassment (#metoo) in the workplace. You are going to see a call for papers on what we call âinvisible inequalities,â meaning societal differences in endowment or access to resources that result in differential opportunities for people within organizations. You are going to see articles by such management thought leaders as Paul Beamish, Ron Burt, Ming-Jer Chen, Gerry Davis, Kathleen Eisenhardt, Ed Freeman, Gerry George, Barry Gerhart, Cristina Gibson, Henrich Greve, Bob Hoskisson, Anita McGahan, John Mathieu, Danny Miller, Rob Ployhart, Jason Shaw, Mo Wang, and Shaker Zahra, among others. You may even see someone from our editorial team as we virtually visit universities via our Editorial World Tour dubbed The Zoom Where It Happens. And all of that is just 2021. I am excited to take over the leadership of the journal and hope to continue to make it so popular that everybody wants a ticket to admission.1
