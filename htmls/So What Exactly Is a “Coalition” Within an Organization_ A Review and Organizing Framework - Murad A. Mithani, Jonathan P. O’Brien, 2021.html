Abstract

An organizational coalition consists of individuals who, despite their persistent differences, work together to pursue a mutually beneficial goal. While central to the political view of the firm, the research on organizational coalitions has evoked diverse characterizations of its members, their relationship with the rest of the organization, and how coalitions balance conflicts and compromise. The result is a fragmented literature that has limited the theoretical clarity necessary to appreciate one of the most important forms of collective political influence in organizations. Drawing on six decades of research, we offer a systematic review of organizational coalitions. We anchor our review on the actors who are party to a coalition, the structures that characterize the boundaries of a coalition, and the processes that lead to a coalition’s influence. This synthesis reveals significant overlaps with regard to the definition of actors, their structural positions, and the perpetual nature of conflict and negotiations. Yet we also note significant divergence in the interactions between actors, how the structure of a coalition maps onto that of an organization, and the processes that affect a coalition’s bargaining position and outcomes. These divergences reveal six distinct streams of research. Using our actor-structure-process model, we organize these research streams into a framework that identifies the diverse conceptualizations as contextual and reconcilable manifestations of a common underlying construct, thus yielding a dynamic model of organizational coalitions. Building on this framework, we propose an agenda for future research.
Although the term coalition has been used extensively in the management literature to refer to individuals with different interests working together in order to increase the odds of achieving some goal, it is a relatively nebulous construct that has come to describe a number of organizational phenomena. In this review, we synthesize the research that has followed the pioneering work on the Behavioral Theory of the Firm (BTF) to understand the primary ways coalitions have been conceptualized. Our starting point is the characteristic that makes coalitions distinct from the other forms of collective influence: conflict.
Conflicts are ubiquitous to human interactions and hence to organizations (Amason, 1996; Eisenhardt, Kahwajy, & Bourgeois, 1997; March, 2006; Pondy, 1967). In the BTF, conflicts emerge from the modeling of an organization as a constellation of distinct interests whereby the key decision maker, the CEO, represents just one such interest, with many more vying to influence organizational choices (Astley, Axelsson, Butler, Hickson, & Wilson, 1982; Augier & March, 2008). These interests—whether directed toward personal gains, beliefs about what is best for the organization, or simply a desire to identify strongly with the organization—make it possible for individuals to form a coalition (Lau & Murnighan, 1998). A coalition, therefore, is a compromise between individuals with overlapping but nonidentical interests undertaken to improve potential gains against others with relatively divergent interests. But the formation of a coalition is not sufficient to overcome conflict; by using the coalition as a political maneuver, organizational actors optimize their gains (Allen, Madison, Porter, Renwick, & Mayes, 1979; Walter, Kellermanns, & Lechner, 2012).
The two most common political pursuits of a coalition include unsanctioned goals and undetermined rules. Coalitions pursue goals that are not formally sanctioned by the organization (Mayes & Allen, 1977). They are not sanctioned either because of the unique or infrequent nature of the goals or because allocative uncertainty makes the associated privileges powerful means by which some can wield their influence over others (Lehman & Ramanujam, 2009). Second, the formation of a coalition can be an attempt to affect the rules of the game—organizational decisions—to make them favorable to its members’ interests (Lyles & Thomas, 1988). This is done in part to gain or maintain influence over goals as well as to ensure the effective functioning of the organization (Ammeter, Douglas, Gardner, Hochwarter, & Ferris, 2002).
The political nature of a coalition distinguishes it from groups and teams. A group is any collection of individuals who share something (even something tangential) in common. A team (or work group) is a collection of individuals sanctioned by the organization and assigned a common goal (Mathieu, Maynard, Rapp, & Gilson, 2008). Unlike a team, whose differences tend to dissipate over time, a coalition ignores internal differences but only as long as the potential for gains surpasses the costs of subjugating self-interests (Harris & Bromiley, 2007). These two considerations—coalition as both a source of and response to conflict—are at the heart of the BTF.
However, despite the growing interest in the study of coalitions as a means of collective political influence in organizations (Gavetti, Greve, Levinthal, & Ocasio, 2012; Hu, He, Blettner, & Bettis, 2017; Reus, Lamont, & Ellis, 2016), as well as several attempts to clarify its conceptualization (Murnighan & Brass, 1991; Stevenson, Pearce, & Porter, 1985), the literature has remained fragmented. A coalition can be an issue-specific collective (March, 1994), an organizational subunit (Pfeffer & Salancik, 2003), a top management team (TMT) (Finkelstein, Hambrick, & Cannella, 2009), or subgroups within the TMT (Kor, 2006); its members may join temporarily or more permanently (Kaplan, 2008); and their interactions can be a source of innovation (Anand, Gardner, & Morris, 2007) and strategic change (Wiersema, 1992), as well as inertia and organizational demise (Sheppard, 1994). These differences point to three unresolved questions that are fundamental to an understanding of collective behavior: Who are the actors in a coalition? How do they structure their activities? And what processes do they engage in? (Macy & Willer, 2002; Marx & Wood, 1975). Our failure to answer these questions has limited the theoretical clarity necessary to understand the “political process that allows decisions to happen without necessarily resolving conflicts among the parties” (Cyert & March, 1992: 229).
We use this review of organizational coalitions to develop a systematic understanding of collective political influence in organizations. Our review centers on the actors who are party to a coalition, the structures that characterize a coalition, and the processes that lead to the coalition’s influence (Kim, Shin, & Jeong, 2016; Lucas, 1987). Our synthesis led us to six different conceptualizations of a coalition, each corresponding to a distinct stream of research. In addition to the stream that follows the BTF, the other streams follow Resource Dependence Theory (RDT), Institutional Theory (IT), Upper Echelon Theory (UET), the Performance Feedback Model (PFM), and research lacking a common theoretical connection that we call Open Stream (OS). We found that each of these streams was unified around a common definition of actors, their structural positions, and the processes that perpetuate conflict and compromises. The literature diverged with regard to interactions and motivations of actors, the structure of the coalitions as they manifest in organizations, and the basis and outcomes of a coalition’s influence and bargaining position. Using our actor-structure-process model, we offer an organizing framework showing that these diverse representations are manifestations of a common concept. We use this framework and incorporate nonorganizational research on coalitions to advance an agenda for future research.
Our study makes three distinct contributions to the literature. First, it illustrates the different ways in which collective political influence operates in organizations. We establish that coalitions are not just reflections of conflicts and compromises but instead represent deeper theoretical conceptualizations that embody identifying assumptions about organizational actors, their internal and external relationships, and the processes of influence and reciprocity that shape decision-making. A clear exposition of these contrasts illustrates the political maneuvers that are salient to each theoretical stream. Second, we show that the divergence among these conceptualizations, while critical, is not a limitation. There are theoretical pathways that allow the conceptualization from one stream of research to be shaped into the conceptualization from another. This integration enables more realistic models of the political environment. Finally, because the concept of coalition is a foundational feature of the various substreams of the BTF, our illustration that divergent conceptualizations of coalitions represent contextual manifestations of a common concept brings us closer to a general theory of organizational coalitions—a theory that holds the promise of an “integrated expression of the Behavioral Theory of the Firm” (Gavetti et al., 2012: 29; also see Dyson, 1976). Our review, we believe, takes an important step in this direction.

Coalitions in Organizations

Conflicts emerge when an actor, whether an individual or a group, perceives its interests are being harmed by another individual or group (Wall & Callister, 1995). Although common within organizations, conflicts are more frequent when disagreements cannot be addressed through established goals and rules (Roche, Teague, & Colvin, 2014). The limited role of hierarchy and structure in the resolution of conflict subjugates the interests of those who occupy less influential roles or positions (Rubin, Pruitt, & Kim, 1994), leading them to political maneuvers to circumvent the constraints (Allen et al., 1979; Walter, Lechner, & Kellermanns, 2008; Yukl & Chavez, 2002). One of the more widely used political maneuvers is the formation of a coalition (Gamson, 1961; Von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944). Coalitions are not political in the sense that they undermine the ideological or operational beliefs of their opponents (Kreutzer, Walter, & Cardinal, 2015). A coalition’s political identity arises from the intent to create an agenda around goals and rules—which can include renegotiations of past agreements—that is not formally sanctioned by the organization (Drory & Romm, 1990; Lechner & Floyd, 2012; Mayes & Allen, 1977). In anticipation of or in response to it, a coalition is frequently greeted by an opposing group, a countercoalition that attempts to negate the former’s gains (Kelley, 1976; Kor, 2006). Although the formation of multiple opposing groups can escalate conflicts, the conflicts that occur within organizational boundaries are more effectively addressed through the process of bargaining and negotiation (Reus et al., 2016). Thus, a study of organizational coalitions is a study of actors with distinct interests jointly engaged in influence processes to increase collective success through activities that are structured to operate independently from organizational goals and rules (Bacharach & Lawler, 1980; Kim et al., 2016; Lucas, 1987; Stevenson et al., 1985; also see Dyson, 1976; Jacobides, MacDuffie, & Tae, 2016).
The concept of organizational coalitions emerged as an intersection of three themes that were foundational to the BTF: game theory, political science, and social psychology (Cyert & March, 1963; March, 1962). The foundation for this intersection was laid earlier in Simon’s effort to broaden the decision context from a unitary agent (an executive decision-maker) to an administrative unit (Simon, 1945) and subsequently through his rejection of the neoclassical notion of hyperrationality in favor of bounded rationality (Simon, 1955). With March and Simon (1958) offering a preliminary integration of these ideas and Thompson (1960, 1961) suggesting their ubiquity, Cyert and March’s (1963) A Behavioral Theory of the Firm brought coalitions to the center of organizational research. However, the central role of coalitions in the BTF is due less to their utility and more to their unavoidability. As Cyert and March (1963) explained, their attempt to understand “the actual process by which individual firms reach decisions” required them to move past the prevailing economic view that saw the firm as little more than an entrepreneur (p. 16). They argued that the decision-making process in organizations is vulnerable to multiple interests. Not only do actors carry different self-interests, these interests include the desire for personal gains, alignment with the interests of others within and outside the organization (Greve & Teh, 2018), as well as beliefs regarding which choices are most appropriate for the organization (Kaplan, 2008). Of course, these considerations are not mutually exclusive (George, 2005). Actors in a coalition may rationalize that the coalition addresses organizational interests when the propositions really serve the self-interests of its members (Lehman & Ramanujam, 2009; Walter et al., 2008). Regardless, self-interests are critical because the potential of fulfilling these interests makes conflict more pervasive, which exacerbates existing differences (March & Simon, 1958).
Such a view of the organizational environment is incompatible with the economic view of the firm as a “cooperative system” with the entrepreneur as its “peak coordinator” (Cyert & March, 1963: 11), a view that ignores the potential for internal frictions (Coase, 1994). As an alternative, the social psychological perspective accounts for actors’ interactions and, in turn, the “reciprocal control possessed by the members of a collectivity” (Thibaut & Kelley, 1959: 4). The latter is often characterized by consensus either because some individuals let go of their preferences and subscribe to those of the others (Allison, 1971) or because deliberations eventually turn disparate interests into a common goal (Kozlowski & Bell, 2003; Mathieu et al., 2008). However, both the economic and the social psychological approaches assume a joint preference ordering.
In contrast, according to the BTF, “[b]asic to the idea of a coalition is the expectation that individual participants in the organization may have substantially different preference orderings (i.e., individual goals)” that are never fully reconciled but only subjugated in anticipation of actual or potential gains (Cyert & March, 1963: 31). An individual is willing to overlook his or her interests in favor of the coalitional goal, and likewise the coalition is willing to make compromises as long as the rewards are proportional (Plambeck & Weber, 2009). By joining together, actors strengthen their capacity and resolve against others’ interests in order to protect their own interests (Bacharach & Lawler, 1980; Nutt, 1998). This allows for a process to reconcile incompatible preference orderings without suppressing or eliminating the underlying differences, making coalitions an effective vehicle for collective political influence in organizations.

Methods

We focused our review on studies that explicitly address the concept of coalitions. Any study that builds on the BTF may be viewed to have coalitions implicit to its arguments even if a coalition is not explicitly cited (see Williamson, 1996). Similarly, while the TMT is often considered a coalition, most research in UET does not treat the TMT as such and instead adopts the lens of behavioral integration that calls for the sharing of “information, resources, and decisions” (Hambrick, 2007: 336). Although including studies that make only implicit references to coalitions could potentially add more insights, doing so runs the risk of making the analysis both intractable and inconsistent with the intended focus of this study. We therefore only included studies that explicitly referenced coalitions (including those that focused on the TMT).
To conduct our analysis, we searched for the term coalition in the nine leading academic journals (i.e., those included in the FT-50 list) that focus on organizational decision-making: Academy of Management Journal, Academy of Management Review, Administrative Science Quarterly, Journal of Management, Journal of Management Studies, Management Science, Organization Science, Organization Studies, and Strategic Management Journal. Initial results revealed that the term coalition appeared in several contexts that are not relevant to our review, such as national or industrial political alliances (e.g., Axelrod, Mitchell, Thomas, Bennett, & Bruderer, 1995). We therefore narrowed our search by requiring the results to include the word coalition in conjunction with reference to Cyert and March. Since a reference to Cyert and March was unlikely to appear in the abstract or keywords, we moved our search to Google Scholar, which has the capacity to scan the full text of academic articles (Martín-Martín, Orduña-Malea, Ayllón, & López-Cózar, 2014; Vine, 2006). This strategy led to the identification of 533 publications, including in-press articles, between 1963 and 2018. Appendix A illustrates the steady growth of research on organizational coalitions. The 5-year moving average trendline illustrates that after the initial surge following publication of A Behavioral Theory of the Firm in 1963, a second wave coincided with the emergence of RDT (Pfeffer & Salancik, 1974), IT (Fligstein, 1993), and UET (Hambrick & Mason, 1984). The activity declined slightly around 1992, most likely because of the popularity of the resource-based view, which presented a more rational model of organizational choice. A recent resurgence started around 2007 and has become more robust with the 50th anniversary of the BTF, largely driven by research on the PFM (Greve, 2003) and in the OS (e.g., Suddaby & Foster, 2017). This timeline illustrates that coalitions have remained a critical construct in some of the most important theories on organizations. Since Google Scholar is not effective in searching within books, we went through the books and chapters cited in the formative articles (discussed below) corresponding to organizational coalitions. This process led to identification of 23 books and 10 book chapters for a total sample of 566 publications.
We began by coding the number of times the term coalition appeared in each of the 533 articles and separated them into three sets: studies that referenced the term more than five times—95 articles (e.g., Stevenson et al., 1985); studies that referenced coalition between two and five times—169 articles (e.g., Nigam, Huising, & Golden, 2016); and those that referenced a coalition only once—269 articles (e.g., Wang, Choi, Wan, & Dong, 2016). Next, we identified the studies that were formative to our understanding of coalitions. One of the authors and two coders went through all the studies independently in chronological order to identify those in which the discussion surrounding a coalition went beyond mere definition. While both authors were well-versed in the literature, none of the coders had prior exposure to the literature and were given only the basic definition of coalition (we used the definition by Polzer, Mannix, & Neale, 1998; see Appendix B). We excluded studies that were dropped by all three coders and included any study for which at least one coder suggested that the discussion had gone beyond a mere definition, reducing the sample to 240 formative studies. The sample included 80 studies (84%) from the first set (coalition > 5 times), 101 studies from the second set (60%), and 59 studies from the third set (22%). Adopting a similar approach, we identified 14 books (e.g., Bacharach & Lawler, 1980) and three book chapters (e.g., Murnighan & Brass, 1991) from the list of 33 (52%). Our review encompasses these 257 (45%) formative studies (identified with an * in the references).
We next translated the three themes—actors, structures, and processes—into specific questions: Who is in conflict with whom? (offering insights into the actors, and their interactions and motivations); How are they organized? (which explicates the structural positions occupied by actors and how the boundaries of a coalition compared to those of the organization); and How do they influence and negotiate with each other? (allowing us to discern the interplay between conflicts and compromises and the resulting implications). These open-ended questions made the synthesis iterative, requiring us to go back and forth across the studies until we reached saturation and no more connections were visible. In the end, we saw a continuum within each theme, revealing unifying dimensions as well as distinctions. The dimensions that united the literature included the definition of actors, their structural positions, and the core processes that explained conflicts and negotiations, highlighting the utility of the concept. Dimensions that revealed inconsistencies included the interactions and motivations of actors, the manifest structure of coalitions in organizations, the influence and bargaining position of coalitions, and their effect on individual, group, and organizational outcomes. Our review begins with the unifying dimensions.

Unifying Dimensions

Actors: Membership and Definition

The literature offered a fairly consistent picture of who forms a coalition. The actors are individuals and groups cooperating against some opposition external to the coalition. While some sources provided precise elaborations that amounted to a clear definition (e.g., Polzer et al., 1998), others were more contextual (e.g., Pfeffer, 1981) or referred to the phenomenon in general (e.g., Bryson & Kelley, 1978). We provide some examples in Appendix B. Overall, these elaborations reveal two patterns. First, they show that the idea behind a coalition has become simpler over time. The focus has been reduced to the coalition’s participants and goals (e.g., Cobb, 2015) rather than the circumstances surrounding its formation (e.g., Pfeffer & Salancik, 1974). Furthermore, the need for limited justification evident in more recent studies suggests that coalitions are now more readily accepted. Second, the key ideas that have been used to describe coalitions have remained largely the same—that is, multiple individuals (or groups) temporarily joining together to achieve a goal. This is surprising because, as we discuss later, the ways in which coalitions are embedded in the theoretical discourse have become increasingly diverse.
The most comprehensive definition of actors and their associations comes from the conceptual review by Stevenson et al. (1985). They offer a clear explanation of what a coalition is (an informal group of interacting individuals), its purpose (individuals are jointly focused on an issue external to the coalition that requires concerted action), how it is formed (deliberately constructed based on mutual interests), and how it relates to the rest of the organization (independent of the formal structure but without its own formal structure and where members, more than nonmembers, are cognizant of its membership). However, this precision is also a limitation, as identifying coalitions that fulfill all these considerations makes it difficult to put this definition into practice. We will return to this limitation at a later point (also see Table 1).

Structure: Positions

Given the informal nature of a coalition (Stevenson et al., 1985), its structure can be discerned only from the positions occupied by its actors (McEvily, Soda, & Tortoriello, 2014). The literature identifies three distinct structural positions with regard to a coalition. The first is the members, who are central to a coalition and shape its agenda. The second is participants, who are part of a coalition but not actively engaged with the issue at hand. The third is potential or neutral members, who are not party to any interests and therefore outside the coalition.
Potential members have neither chosen sides in a conflict nor made any commitments (Zhang & Greve, 2019), but due to their association with the organization, they are viable candidates for recruitment (Kim, Shin, Oh, & Jeong, 2007). Until then, they are neutral to all sides in a conflict (March, 1962). Once having made a commitment to join a coalition, the neutral member’s potential is realized and is on the way to becoming a participant. The commitment can take various forms, including cognitive, verbal, or behavioral (DiBenigno, 2017; Ocasio, 1994). Once a commitment is made, it is generally unambiguous—known to participants as well as to others in the coalition (Stevenson et al., 1985)—although opposing coalitions may not always be aware that a commitment has been made (Bettenhausen & Murnighan, 1985; Polzer et al., 1998; also see Von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944). A commitment, however, does not mean that participants are actively engaged in all activities of the coalition. They can avoid active participation by either failing to engage in debates (Eisenhardt & Bourgeois, 1988)—possibly because they are unavailable when such debates happen (Ganz, 2018)—or by giving equal support to conflicting perspectives (Greve & Zhang, 2017; Plambeck & Weber, 2009). A participant becomes an actual member by successfully demonstrating support for a position or a side in a conflict—which is when a concerted action begins to take shape and membership is established (e.g., Denis, Dompierre, Langley, & Rouleau, 2011). When individuals have the option to join multiple coalitions (Murnighan & Brass, 1991), they can simultaneously be potential members in some, participants in others, and members in still other coalitions (Mintzberg, 1983).

Processes: Conflict and Negotiation

Fundamental to the discussion of coalitions are two opposing processes: conflict and negotiation.

Perpetual conflict

The perpetual nature of conflict represents “a continuous bargaining-learning process” (Cyert & March, 1963: 33) that mostly occurs between coalitions (Hu et al., 2017; March, 1962) but sometimes within a coalition as well (Knight et al., 1999). Not only is there a conflict to start with (Pfeffer, 1981), but coalitions also perpetuate the conflict instead of converging to shared interests or an identical worldview. Quite often, it is not an actual response but the perceived effectiveness of response patterns that becomes a point of departure (Greve, 2003). One exception is when individuals do not have a guiding template based on past experiences, in which case members are encouraged to jointly structure an alternative response that is well-equipped to deal with impending challenges (Bettenhausen & Murnighan, 1985).
The persistent conflict experienced by organizational coalitions does not conform to an image of dramatic showdowns (although see Daudi, 1986; DiBenigno, 2017). Rather, the collaboration and teamwork prized by organizations make conflicts more subtle and largely hidden (Hayward & Boeker, 1998; Kolb & Bartunek, 1991). They surface in the form of minor but persistent differences specific to issues, contexts, routines, or outcomes—such as disagreements about responsibility, diverse outlooks on strategic decisions, or endless negotiations over corporate resources (Cruz, Gómez-Mejia, & Becerra, 2010; Hoskisson, Chirico, Zyung, & Gambeta, 2017; Schwochau, Feuille, & Delaney, 1988). The conflicts are mostly known to parties on the opposite sides of the negotiation and to others who may discern the conflict by the consistent inclination of individuals toward or away from a specific group. Even these inclinations are sometimes less visible, and it may be silence that betrays an actor’s interest or allegiance (March, 1994).
Negotiations
A process that accompanies perpetual conflict but works in the opposite direction is the distribution of incentives. The literature is consistent in acknowledging the important role played by incentives in the formation and maintenance of coalitions. Originally conceived as a payoff (distribution) function in game theory (Von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944), incentives for participation in a coalition were introduced in the BTF as side payments. They appear in various forms, including money, personal treatment, authority, interest, love, perquisites, private commitments (Cyert & March, 1963), lighter workloads, reduced supervision (Manns & March, 1978), prestige, title, awards (Pfeffer, 1981), budgets, allocation of functions, organizational precedents (Stevenson et al., 1985), adaptive aspiration (Hu et al., 2017), and more often, policy commitments (March, 1962). It is this latter consideration that has the most influence on organizational goals (Greve, 2008). These agreements are shaped around either an immediate distribution of a reward, a repayment of past favors, or a promise for future payoffs. They make side payments amenable to renegotiation, which is central to the political view of organizations (Bacharach & Lawler, 1980; March, 1962; Pfeffer & Salancik, 2003).
Side payments are the glue that holds a coalition together. They do so by performing two primary functions. First, side payments help the members of a coalition buy the loyalty of others whose support can benefit their goals. Second, side payments serve as rewards that encourage existing members to maintain allegiance to the coalition. However, the utility of side payments is limited. Their effect does not last long and the associated agreements survive only as long as the payments persist or the promise of future payoffs remains credible (Klein, Mahoney, McGahan, & Pitelis, 2018; Ocasio, 1994). A decrease in the credibility of a coalition is likely to impose a higher discount on its commitment to others, requiring proportionately greater distribution of rewards in the future (Bacharach & Lawler, 1980).
Our discussion of side payments raises a critical question: Where do the side payments come from? They are associated with past, current, or future resources that exceed what is absolutely necessary for the operation of the organization—that is, slack. That is, while slack can be deployed to stabilize organizational operations (Greve, 2003), the stability is in part due to slack’s distribution as a side payment to gain and maintain the allegiance of coalition members (Zhang & Greve, 2019). Greater access to slack can increase a coalition’s attractiveness to others, improve the odds of bargains leaning in its favor, and allow it to take a more central role in setting organizational goals (Greenwood & Hinings, 1988; Hambrick & Mason, 1984).

Inconsistent Dimensions

Despite the overlaps discussed above, the broader theoretical discourse revealed significant inconsistencies that point toward divergent perspectives of actors’ interactions and motivations, how the structure of the coalition relates to that of the organization, conditions that attenuate conflict and negotiations, and the resulting outcomes. These inconsistencies manifest in a distinct view of coalitions through each of the theoretical lenses highlighted in Appendix A. In addition to the BTF (44% studies), these include RDT (9%), IT (5%), UET (16%), PFM (7%), and OS (19%).

BTF coalition

The coalitions in the BTF emerged from observations surrounding decision-making processes in organizations (Cyert, Dill, & March, 1958; Cyert & March, 1959; Cyert, March, & Starbuck, 1961; Cyert, Simon, & Trow, 1956), culminating, as we discussed earlier, in a foundational assumption in A Behavioral Theory of the Firm (Cyert & March, 1963). The BTF’s coalitions represent lasting differences between individual preferences that carry through in the organization and are particularly visible when key decisions are under consideration (Pondy, 1967). The coalitions are not permanent; they shift and reformulate around issues and preferences. However, in order to arrive at a generalizable theory, Cyert and March left many questions unanswered, such as who participates in a coalition or which issues are more important for a coalition. Although March’s later work refined some of these ideas—for example, by highlighting complementarity in coalition formation that leads to complete congruence whereby all members “want the same policy” or to complete indifference (logrolling) whereby individuals “are indifferent to each other’s demands” (March, 1994: 151-159)—the emphasis remained on their contextual presence rather than on completeness.

RDT coalition

Pfeffer and Salancik’s work on RDT popularized “the coalitional view of organizations” (Pfeffer & Salancik, 2003). In contrast to the BTF, where coalitions are drawn around issues, RDT’s coalitions are formed around “the differences in objectives and preferences of subunits and participants” (Salancik & Pfeffer, 1974: 454). Subunits could include academic departments in universities as well as the more traditional economic functions in firms such as sales, finance, manufacturing, etc. (Pfeffer & Salancik, 2003). RDT views the function most salient to organizational independence as the more dominant coalition, and the influence of this coalition is contingent on its ability to shelter the organization from environmental uncertainty. Although internal members are sometimes aligned with external participants (Greve & Zhang, 2017; Shu & Lewin, 2017), the focus is primarily on coalitional dynamics that are internal to the organization (Pfeffer, 1981).

IT coalition

IT extended the RDT coalition to an institutional concept by capitalizing on Fligstein’s (1985: 384) argument that the “top leaders in the organization and their origins in the firm are thus a good measure of what department or function is in control of the firm.” Unlike RDT, IT views the control exerted by a function or subunit as contingent not on its capacity to reduce external dependence but on the conceptions of control embraced by the broader institutional environment (Klein et al., 2018; also see Tolbert & Zucker, 1983). The beliefs are not directly visible, and institutional legitimacy is discerned from the functional association of the CEO (Fligstein, 1993). Nonrepresented coalitions can exert their influence through participants in the TMT and the board of directors (Ocasio, 1994; Ocasio & Kim, 1999).

UET coalition

One of the most popular conceptions of coalitions emerges from Hambrick and Mason’s (1984) work on the organizational elite—the dominant coalition comprising the TMT. It changes the discussion of the distribution of influence by shifting the focus from the actors (i.e., the functional unit or the CEO) to the stage (i.e., the hierarchical level). Although UET does not identify the nondominant groups, its focus on the top executives suggests that the entire rest of the organization is the nondominant coalition (Greve & Mitsuhashi, 2007). This approach eliminates the discussion of conflict to favor consensus and behavioral integration (Hambrick, 1994; also see Hambrick et al., 2007: 336), although there is some recognition that executive backgrounds or perspectives may at times propagate intra-TMT differences (Knight et al., 1999; also see Mooney, Holahan, & Amason, 2007).

PFM coalition

A more recent conceptualization follows Greve’s work on problemistic and slack search (Greve, 2003). It incorporates the resource dependencies of RDT and the institutionalized beliefs of IT but mostly in the face of performance constraints or excesses (Gomez-Mejia, Patel, & Zellweger, 2018). In particular, coalitions and their conflicts are more visible in the face of performance shortfalls when divergent perspectives become a liability to organizational change (Desai, 2016) and with superior performance that imbues organizational goals with cultural beliefs (O’Brien & David, 2014). In this model, coalitions are internal groups responsible for shaping aspirations (Harris & Bromiley, 2007; Hu et al., 2017), but they also are highly responsive to the expectations of external coalitions representing stakeholders (O’Brien & David, 2014; Zhang & Greve, 2019).

OS coalition

Many more studies refer to organizational coalitions but do not have much in common except for the absence of an overarching framework. That is, while they refer to Cyert and March, neither the BTF nor any of the other research streams discussed above are central to their theorizing (e.g., Ford, 1980). These studies further divide into two subgroups. The first includes studies that have avoided any a priori conceptualization and in which membership and boundaries of a coalition are discerned empirically, revealing active conflict (Eisenhardt & Bourgeois, 1988; Ezzamel, 1994). The second subgroup has relied on a priori conceptualization that is limited to the CEO and other senior executives (Kreutzer et al., 2015) and occasionally the board to explain passive conflict (Pearce, 1995; Pearce & DeNisi, 1983). In all, they present several arguments for intercoalition conflict, including functional distinction (DiBenigno, 2017; Jensen & Zajac, 2004), hierarchical status (Pearce, 1995; Pearce & DeNisi, 1983), tenure (Daudi, 1986), communication (Murnighan & Brass, 1991; Polzer et al., 1998), or some combination of these factors (Eisenhardt & Bourgeois, 1988), while side payments are almost nonexistent. The willingness of the coalition’s members to escalate (Dye, Eggers, & Shapira, 2014) and overcome (DiBenigno, 2017) differences despite an absence of rewards suggests that their ideologies represent worldviews and not self-interests. Although the focus of OS is also limited largely to the organizational elite (Jensen & Zajac, 2004), it recognizes this as an artifact of the sampling process and not of influence processes (Eisenhardt & Bourgeois, 1988).
Next, we review how the differences among these research streams lead to inconsistencies in the conceptualization of coalitions in terms of their actors, structure, and processes.

Actors: Interactions and Motivations

Despite general agreement on the types of actors, there are inconsistencies in how the actors are seen in terms of interactions and motivations. The inconsistencies are readily visible when we consider the attributes highlighted by Stevenson et al. (1985). Although none of the six streams satisfies all eight attributes (discussed earlier), as shown in Table 1, they vary in different ways.
While BTF, RDT, PFM, and OS theorize extensive actor interactions within and between coalitions (Desai, 2016; Pfeffer & Leong, 1977; Polzer et al., 1998; Pondy, 1967), UET focuses only on interactions within the TMT (Finkelstein et al., 2009), and IT considers only interactions between the CEO and the aligned and—to a limited extent—unaligned subunits, but not within the unaligned subunits (Fligstein, 1993; Kim et al., 2007). The motivations behind the formation of a coalition are also divergent. While the BTF recognizes the multiple ways in which actors forge relationships around an issue, formal organizational structure plays a limited role in shaping interactions (Baldridge, 1971; Deb, David, & O’Brien, 2017). This idea is also visible in OS from the nature of interactions, the quality of arguments, and prior experiences or demographics that are less responsive to hierarchical influence (Denis et al., 2011; Polzer et al., 1998). In contrast, RDT’s and IT’s focus on subunits suggests that coalition formation is motivated by organizational structure, making the actors and their membership more permanent (Klein et al., 2018) and issues (Dalton, Daily, Johnson, & Ellstrand, 1999) or institutional beliefs (Fligstein, 1993) more likely to exacerbate the differences. In UET, the status boundary (i.e., TMT) shapes the coalition, discouraging issue-specific membership or vertical interactions between TMT and non-TMT coalitions (Williams, Chen, & Agarwal, 2017). Within the PFM, coalitions with board members (Desai, 2016) or external owners (Zhang & Greve, 2019) also impose a constraint on the independence of interactions from the formal structure.
Despite treatment of coalitions as informal groups, there is significant disagreement on the extent to which the actors are considered equal. IT’s recognition of the CEO as the most powerful and representative unit of the prevailing coalition (Ocasio, 1994), UET’s recognition of status-based differences (Hambrick & Mason, 1984), and OS’s observation that the CEO or supervisor often has the last word (Dye et al., 2014; Godkin & Montano, 1991) suggest that despite the absence of a formal internal structure, hierarchical considerations occasionally creep into negotiations. A similar inconsistency appears in actors’ recognition of others who are part of their coalition. Discussions assume but rarely theorize whether members know and interact with all others in their coalition, which is difficult due to the presence of large external coalitions in RDT (Salancik, 1979; Shu & Lewin, 2017) or a large set of external investors in the PFM (O’Brien & David, 2014; Zhang & Greve, 2019), and even more unlikely in the BTF, which treats all potential parties that interact with the organization as a potential coalition (Cyert & March, 1992). Although internal actors may have notions of coalition membership, it is unlikely they are fully aware of all external members, just as it is unlikely that external coalitions invest time and resources in identifying internal members, which limits the mutual perception of membership. Furthermore, while external focus is often the reason for coalition formation, the BTF’s view of coalitions within coalitions (discussed in the next section) and the PFM’s focus on adaptive aspirations suggest that actors may at times be consumed by internal goals (Hu et al., 2017). Recognizing that members’ interactions are constrained and they might not even be aware of all the other members, concerted action becomes a function of well-organized relationships. But this is unlikely in the BTF where some coalitions, such as suppliers, may be hesitant to act despite agreeing in principle with others. The potential for concerted action appears more likely in UET because of its lower vulnerability to internal rifts (Dess, 1987), and in RDT and IT because of subunit identification, but less so in OS, where some members are more visible and others unavailable for action (Ezzamel, 1994).
In sum, things that appear relatively consistent with regard to the actors are the lack of a structure that dictates interactions, their focus on issues that are mostly external to the coalition, and to some extent, their willingness to act in concert. All other attributes identified by Stevenson et al. (1985) exhibit significant differences. Actors in a coalition may have extensive as well as limited interactions; they may come together deliberately or be influenced by the hierarchical structure; they may focus on consistent long-term challenges or an immediate issue; and they may or may not have a mutually shared perception of membership (for a discussion of how a coalition differs from an alliance or a partnership between individuals, see Appendix C).

Structure: Configuration and Boundaries

Divergent characterizations are particularly visible when moving beyond a coalition’s internal structure to examine its boundaries relative to those of the organization. These differences actually are embedded in the foundational work of the BTF. Cyert and March (1963) acknowledged the difficulty of clearly specifying a coalition and explained that “[d]rawing the boundaries of an organizational coalition once and for all is impossible” (p. 27), leading to a conceptualization that expanded as the theory dealt with distinct organizational challenges.
Cyert and March (1963: 27) began with a simple conceptualization that treated the “organization as a coalition. It is a coalition of individuals, some of them organized into sub-coalitions.” They used the terms “members,” “subgroup,” or “subunit” for subcoalitions (pp. 27, 37, 100, 115, 276), “class” for a group of subcoalitions (pp. 27 and 30), “participants” for individuals (p. 270), and “peak coordinator” for the topmost executive (p. 11). Added to this, March (1962) considered the topmost executive as an “executive-political broker” (p. 674), preferred the term “coalition” over “subcoalition” (pp. 673-674), and in turn, equated coalitions to “interest groups” (p. 671). While IT views the external influence more critically than either RDT or PFM, enforcing agents in the external environment are largely ignored (Fligstein, 1993). Instead, the internal coalitions actively seek to align themselves to perceived efficiency beliefs, raising the prospect of confrontation with the executive broker, the CEO (Kim et al., 2016). Potential internal coalitions that are otherwise dormant include disenchanted members of the board, executives from nondominant subunits, and possibly others (Kim et al., 2007; Ocasio, 1994). This configuration is more consistent with the BTF’s Structure 3. In contrast, UET offers the simplest structure. Compared to IT, where the executive broker maintains a distinct identity, UET treats the CEO and the TMT as unified and the only visible coalition (Finkelstein et al., 2009). Middle management and other staff presumably represent one or more potential coalitions that rarely appear on the scene. This formulation also corresponds to the BTF’s Structure 3, albeit weakly.
In the OS, two different structures emerge. The discussion of active conflict presents a structure consistent with the BTF’s Structure 4, where coalition formation is contingent on the issue at hand, absent external influence (Eisenhardt & Bourgeois, 1988; Nutt, 1998). Conversely, studies on passive conflict, relying on more stable classification criteria (expertise, survey responses, etc.) correspond to the BTF’s Structure 1 (Kreutzer et al., 2015; Pearce, 1995). The differences in structure, in turn, reveal unique perspectives on conflicts and negotiations.

Processes: Basis and Outcomes of Conflicts and Negotiations

The consistency in the literature regarding the perpetual nature of conflict and negotiations disintegrates when we examine the processes that explain the differences (nature and escalation of conflict), the means deployed by coalitions to bargain for their position (resolution strategies and interdependencies), and the implications for individuals, groups, and the organization.

Nature of conflict

The BTF views conflict as a manifestation of individual self-interests shaped by “a prior structure of preferences” that is in place before an individual joins an organization (March & Simon, 1958: 65; also see Cohen, 2007). Participation in a coalition is therefore a manifestation rather than a source of these preferences (Jain, 2016). Individual differences in perspectives can be reinforced through association with like-minded individuals and suppressed in the presence of conflicting preferences but rarely go away altogether (Ganz, 2018). However, conflict in the BTF, over time, occurs among different coalitions and on different issues. The persistence of conflict does not suggest that the same members are consistently on the opposite sides, and some may even stop participating (Cobb, 2015).
In RDT, where coalitions are structured around functional units, conflict identifies differences in perspectives due to expertise and skills (Salancik & Pfeffer, 1974), reputation (Pfeffer & Moore, 1980), and networks (Pfeffer & Leong, 1977). Instead of the BTF’s reliance on deep-seated preferences, RDT’s focus is on evident identities. It sees subunit affiliation as a socialization that shapes individual preferences (Friedkin & Simpson, 1985). This view is also represented in IT but with two major changes. First, conflict is associated with socially validated opinions and beliefs, and individual expertise and skills orchestrate rather than embody the prevailing beliefs (Fligstein, 1993). Second, in contrast to RDT where the most influential subunit dominates, institutional forces can allow a number of efficiency beliefs to coexist (Ocasio & Kim, 1999). This suggests that intercoalition conflicts can diffuse over time once each coalition recognizes that another’s visibility does not jeopardize its own.
The PFM embraces a more generalized view of conflict in which internal interests are critical (Hu et al., 2017) but also instruments for external influence (Zhang & Greve, 2019). They are jointly geared toward shaping organizational goals around the coalition members’ shared economic identity. This identity can be informed by institutional beliefs (Zhang & Greve, 2019), societal values (O’Brien & David, 2014), or the desire for emotional well-being (Gomez-Mejia et al., 2018). The conflicts can diffuse due to a firm’s ability to fulfill its aspirations, changing the issue but not a coalition’s association with the issue (Harris & Bromiley, 2007).
In OS, the executive’s ideological view of organizational actions shapes conflict (Denis et al., 2011) often due to a challenge to his or her identity (DiBenigno, 2017). Thus, conflict is personal, tests an individual’s worldview, and yields consistent resistance (Walter et al., 2008). Given the social nature of organizations and the tactical nature of coalitions, conflict may not reveal itself in every social encounter. Yet it persists and becomes visible intermittently (Lechner & Floyd, 2012; Polzer et al., 1998). Even though the focus of OS has largely been on top management, it recognizes that members lower in the hierarchy who closely subscribe to an executive subgroup may rally around the coalition’s ideology (Litschert & Bonham, 1978; Schilit, 1987). Hence, intra-TMT conflict may diffuse to lower levels (Dye et al., 2014).
UET’s focus on hierarchy as the marker of a coalition boundary stands out because its conceptualization of conflict is vertical, between high-status organizational elites and low-status others. However, it is unclear if status per se is the mechanism driving conflict or, in line with Thompson (1967) and Tushman (1977), whether the status-induced broadening of executive perspective leads to an alternate view of organizational choices (Hambrick & Mason, 1984). In any case, the conflict appears stationary. It cannot be ruled out, however, that diminishing status differences at lower levels may give way to alternate sources of conflict (a possibility that remains to be studied), thus turning them into horizontal misalignments as in the other streams.
Escalation of conflict
The discussion of the nature of conflict points to a difference in circumstances that leads to an escalation of conflict. In the BTF, conflict escalates because of the constraint on resources sought by the participants, materializing as contention over issues: whether or not to pursue a path of action (Baldridge, 1971), search for more effective alternatives (Ganz, 2018), or determine how to respond to external and internal threats (Deb et al., 2017). Conflicts precede a decision and trigger bargaining and negotiation, including side payments. The conditions of conflict in RDT are similar to those in the BTF with two exceptions: First, conflicts escalate in the face of external uncertainty (Jemison, 1981); second, the focus is predominantly on decisions that have compromises built into them, such as budgets or resource allocations (Hills & Mahoney, 1978). To some extent, contentious decisions represent bargains around group-level benefits (in contrast with side payments, which are specific to individuals). Thus, participants diverge in their perspectives toward the value of a decision not just for themselves but also for their coalition.
In IT, conflict emerges as a joint effect of changes in institutional beliefs, at times accompanied by the weakening influence of the dominant coalition. Thus, the external and internal conditions that separately affect BTF coalitions become integrated into one (Ocasio, 1994; also see Greve & Zhang, 2017). Consistent with the BTF, the conflict is viewed over time in the unfolding of efficiency beliefs—a process rather than an event (Palmer, Jennings, & Zhou, 1993). In the PFM, conflict manifests in the face of a discrepancy between performance and aspirations, and it limits the potential for change (Desai, 2016). Thus, coalitions that are not aligned with a decision become a constraint on organizational action (Hu et al., 2017), suggesting that the negotiations do not go far enough, particularly in the face of adversity.
OS has looked at the escalation of conflict in two ways. In the first, conflict in relationships (Eisenhardt & Bourgeois, 1988) or routines (Kreutzer et al., 2015) remains a persistent threat regardless of any trigger. In the second, the focus on controversial decisions suggests that conflict is heightened (Provan, 1991) to reinforce ideological positions and delay strategic decisions (Dye et al., 2014). In UET, since coalitions other than the TMT are absent, conflict is unaffected by changes in the internal or external environments, although conflict does appear occasionally within the TMT (Kor, 2006; Lyles, 1987).
Conflict resolution
We identified five instruments of conflict resolution that appeared critical, although they were used differently in each stream. In addition to negotiation, they include avoidance, force, mediation, and—when all else fails—a change in the members. While the discussion of conflict in the BTF centers on negotiation, it expands to include force in RDT. Those in power use their influence to maintain dominance (Welbourne & Trevor, 2000), and when this fails, they open the doors for negotiation (Pfeffer, 1981). In IT, the primary means of conflict resolution is avoidance, because the CEO’s institutional legitimacy makes it quite difficult for minority groups to gain sufficient power (Kim et al., 2007; Klein et al., 2018). When they do, however, negotiations buy the dominant coalition only a limited amount of time at the helm before the impending departure of more influential members (Ocasio, 1994). The PFM associates conflict resolution with changes in members, either by reducing the number of conflicting perspectives or making it more likely that like-minded individuals will join the negotiations (Desai, 2016; Hu et al., 2017). Absent member turnover, conflict persists as long as conditions persist. UET treats organizational elites as indomitable by the lower level staff, suggesting force as the only response (e.g., Hambrick & D’Aveni, 1992), and OS views negotiation to be the norm with occasional reference to the mediating role of third parties (DiBenigno, 2017; Pearce, 1995).
Interdependencies
Regard for negotiations recognizes some level of interdependence between coalitions (Welsh & Slusher, 1986), either equal or unequal. In turn, bargains facilitated by interdependence may be reciprocated by both sides or may remain largely one-sided. In the BTF, interdependencies are specific to the issue at hand (Manns & March, 1978). Coalitions are continuously in negotiation and always attentive to the preferences and behind-the-scenes maneuvering of the other groups. Each coalition is dependent on the reciprocity of others (Lowe, 1971), and given issue-specific influence, they are more or less equal during negotiations. Similar to the BTF, OS recognizes that competing coalitions are more equal in influence (Ford, 1980), suggesting a balanced and reciprocal relationship (Walter et al., 2008).
In RDT, interdependence represents established heterogeneities in influence that are directed from the more dominant to the less dominant coalitions (Xia, Wang, Lin, Yang, & Li, 2018), making their relationships inherently unequal. However, the dominant coalition is not immune to the influence of others. Insofar as others seek the support of the dominant group for security and survival, the dominant coalition seeks the support of others to maintain its privileges (Pfeffer, 1981), making the relationship reciprocal.
IT presents a more confrontational and dynamic model of interdependence. The CEO’s dependence on other factions is limited, since it is the efficiency ideal that brings him or her to power (Ocasio & Kim, 1999). It begins with a shared belief of all key executives and directors that empowers the CEO and encourages loyalty and is substantiated by reciprocity only to a limited extent (Kim et al., 2007, 2016). However, as the CEO experiences obsolescence of expertise and networks, the balance shifts, making interdependencies unequal but in the opposite direction and less favorable to the CEO (Ocasio, 1994). The overall dynamic suggests an unequal and largely nonreciprocal interdependency. In contrast, the PFM sees interdependence as equal in general, whether between coalitions that are internal or external to the firm (Greve, 2003). But equality is not sustained at all times (see Tuggle, Sirmon, Reutzel, & Bierman, 2010). A challenge to organizational adaptation unearths internal conflicts, suggesting that favors and side payments are less applicable during these periods—or perhaps it is the diminishing access to side payments that exacerbates conflicts (Desai, 2016). This makes reciprocity a less reliable means for forging coalition agreements (Gomez-Mejia et al., 2018).
In UET, in spite of organizational participants’ dependence on the dominant coalition for organizational and personal performance, the lack of visibility of nondominant coalitions presents a highly asymmetric model of interdependence. The TMT is immune to any influence emanating from the lower levels, making the relationship unequal and nonreciprocal (Finkelstein et al., 2009). Outcomes
In examining the implications of coalitions, studies have focused on individual, group, and organizational outcomes (i.e., as dependent variables) differently depending on whether the emphasis is on conflict (Hayward & Boeker, 1998), strategies to resolve conflict (Kreutzer et al., 2015), or qualitatively neutral interactions between coalitions (Wei, Chiang, & Wu, 2012). Although firm-level outcomes have been the primary focus, individual- and group-level implications have also received considerable attention. Coalitions: An Organizing Framework

The preceding synthesis suggests that a single conceptualization of organizational coalitions is not possible and likely not even desirable, as different types of coalitions are the foci of different streams of research. As Gavetti et al. (2012: 29) observed, “the Behavioral Theory of the Firm has existed as a kind of open system with respect to the broader field of management. Elements, such as . . . the firm as a political coalition, are refined and further developed but to a significant degree have speciated from the broader structure and content of the Behavioral Theory of the Firm itself.” One way to approach these conceptual inconsistencies, we believe, is to treat them as alternate forms of collective political influence in organizations. The theoretical distance between any two streams can then be viewed as a representation of the number of changes necessary to transform the coalition from one stream of research to another. To this end, we reduced Table 2 to the fewest possible distinctions, either as an increase or a decrease in theoretical emphasis, that can help reshape one stream’s conceptualization of the coalition to fit another’s. For our starting point, we use the BTF coalition that, because of its pioneering role, makes it easier to see its ties with the other streams.
As shown in Table 4, our review suggests six theoretical modifications that can turn the BTF coalition into a RDT coalition. The first is the change in actors, requiring greater influence of organizational structure in coalition membership (e.g., subunits). This modification leads to structurally a more permanent coalition than the issue-specific coalitions in the BTF. At the same time, this change deemphasizes the role of the executive broker. The processes necessary for this transformation include an emphasis on environmental uncertainty, a connection between this uncertainty and a specific set of skills or expertise, and the deployment of those skills to shape the distribution of power between organizational actors.

The transformation of the BTF coalition into an IT coalition is theoretically the most demanding. Of the 10 modifications, 4 are identical to those in RDT: membership influenced by organizational structure, a more permanent coalition, a greater emphasis on skills, and their utility in distorting the internal distribution of power. Additional modifications in the actors include theoretical emphasis on the CEO (thereby accentuating the role of the executive broker) and the board members and a decrease in the frequency of interactions within nondominant groups. Theoretical changes in the processes include a focus on efficiency beliefs as the basis of conflicts, acknowledgment that changes in internal distribution of power respond to changes in coalition members, and a decrease in the emphasis on negotiation as a means to diffuse conflict.
The seven modifications that turn a BTF into a UET coalition share the two common elements from above: Actors are influenced by the organizational structure, and the structure of the coalition is permanent. In addition, theoretical emphasis on actors is limited to the TMT, and interactions between the TMT and others in the organization are less critical. This leads the coalition to be structured around hierarchical status, increasing the emphasis on processes that account for actors’ demographics and reducing emphasis on conflict to favor consensus.
Of the four changes needed for a BTF coalition to morph into a PFM coalition, one is more common—the influence of organizational structure on the coalition membership. A second shift, the possibility that replacement of one or more coalition members can reshape conflict, is similar to one of the process changes that synthesize BTF and IT coalitions. In addition, the structure of a PFM coalition, relative to the BTF coalition, requires a greater emphasis on external stakeholders such as investors, who are more influential during conflict and resolution processes when performance assessments are markedly different from aspirations. Finally, the transformation of a BTF coalition to an OS coalition is the least demanding. It requires three essential modifications: The actors are reduced to subgroups within the TMT, the board of directors, or functional units; the process of bargaining is seen to be influenced by actors’ ideological positions; and finally, the emphasis on ideological position limits the viability of side payments to forge compromises.
The organizing framework illustrates that coalitions are neither static nor fixed. Once we recognize the organizational context, it is possible to identify the coalition that is most likely to emerge under those circumstances, thus presenting the distinct conceptualizations as theoretical contingencies. These contingencies represent contextual (i.e., organizational conditions and perceptions about the external environment) and temporal evolutions in actors, structures, and processes that offer a dynamic view of organizational coalitions. This view takes into account the “shifts in coalitions” (March, 1962: 678), which gets us closer to a more general theory of organizational coalitions. Such a general theory can not only integrate the various substreams of the BTF, it can also offer a realistic modeling of the political environment that neither places constraints on theoretical development when accommodating empirical evidence nor is constrained by theoretical boundaries. To that end, we highlight the critical gaps in our understanding and unanswered questions that offer important avenues for future research.

Future Research

We see the avenues for future research in two ways: as an extension of organizational research as well as a broader expansion that capitalizes on the vast research on coalitions outside of organizational studies. We start with the possibilities for extension.
In terms of the actors, while we know who they are and how to identify them (subunits, executives, etc.), the critical issue of coalition formation remains unclear. Two of the most important considerations in the formation of coalitions include identification of potential allies and assessment of the costs of participation (Seidmann & Winter, 1998). Foremost, to what extent are coalitions structured more around familiarity than mutual interests? One way to answer this question is to observe members’ interactions during and in the absence of conflicts, where the PFM offers a valuable contingency. Since conflicts in the PFM are associated with performance-aspiration discrepancy, if activities associated with coalitions persist even when the firm maintains its relative position, we may uncover some of the social dimensions underlying coalition formation. Second, what are the personal and emotional costs of participating in a coalition? One possibility is to take into account organizational tenure as a contingency. Organizational members with a longer tenure are more likely to experience emotional costs when they are on the opposite sides of a negotiation. Members’ prior relationships and shared experiences, through the lens of UET as well as of OS, can reveal if such conflicts are promptly settled or leave emotional scars.
A related concern is the structural environment that encompasses actors. While we know that organizational goals are influenced by internal bargains, it is unclear if changes in the structure of coalitions have a similar effect. To start, we can follow the changes in coalition structure as a predictor of shifts in organizational goals. Such an investigation would require the identification of a coalition that experiences changes in its boundaries. This can extend some of the earlier work in the BTF (Baldridge, 1971) and the more recent research in OS (DiBenigno, 2017). A related question pertains to coalition’s internal structure. Even though coalitions are devoid of formal coordination hierarchies, do all members have the same influence? Given the potential hierarchies and differences in executive power examined in UET (Finkelstein, 1992; Priem, 1990), it offers a promising path to understand the heterogeneities in internal influence.
In regard to processes, our understanding of coalitional bargains is quite limited and side payments remain a black box. In the BTF, a problem between two individuals is resolved by one making a side payment to the other (or to a related third party whose involvement can strengthen one’s position), turning them into a coalition. When this pair has a conflict with a third individual, more side payments are made and the coalition expands. Progressive theorizing eventually turns the whole organization into a coalition. A limitation of this argument is that when more and more participants are added into the coalition, the benefits to its existing members decrease, thus eroding their commitment (March, 1994; Riker, 1962). In fact, the progressive coalitions in the BTF, while aspiring for stability, introduce instability by threatening the payoffs to its existing members. Hence, an understanding of side payments needs to start from studying their perceived value to existing and potential members: Namely, to what extent is it a failure to accumulate slack versus the coalition’s failure to effectively distribute side payments that contributes to organizational demise? Since RDT and IT both offer significant insights into how the dominant coalition gains, maintains, and eventually loses power, they may broaden our understanding of the role of side payments, possibly by examining the utility of slack in the hands of various coalitions. Moreover, with slack facilitating search (Greve, 2003), it is likely to be associated with inflated side payments to internal coalitions, thus identifying contexts where side payments can be more visible.

Nonorganizational Research

Nonorganizational research offers an excellent opportunity to broaden this discussion, as coalitions have been extensively studied in other contexts. A starting point could be the areas that influenced Cyert, March, and Simon’s theorizing: game theory, social psychology, and political science. For example, game theory is increasingly cognizant of the need to incorporate actors’ beliefs into bargaining situations (Gintis, 2014). It would be interesting to examine the extent to which these beliefs correspond to self-interests as in BTF or institutional beliefs in IT. This could be a valuable opportunity since both these streams are open to laboratory studies where participant profiles offer detailed insights into the structure of underlying beliefs (e.g., Schilke, 2018; Segev, 1987). Moreover, research in political science suggests that growing extremism in political positions is due to the increasing diversity of constituents as well as to greater leader oversight over party operations (Layman, Carsey, & Horowitz, 2006). This can complement the discussion of beliefs by identifying if a diverse workforce and CEO engagement, as examined in OS (Anand et al., 2007), increases the role of self-interests and efficiency beliefs during interactions.
Research in social psychology also offers some suggestions on the incorporation of actor’s beliefs, stemming from the discussion of an external locus of control. A growing recognition of the role of institutions has made individuals more aware of the constraints on free-will. It has encouraged a self-serving bias that leads coalitions to associate conflicts with events beyond their control (Twenge, Zhang, & Im, 2004). Could this mean that coalitions are less inclined to accept their limitations and likely to escalate conflicts in organizations surrounded by highly developed institutions? The answer, we believe, lies in understanding how executive biases are reshaped by changes in institutional environments, possibly by integrating UET with the discussion of organizational errors (Crossland & Hambrick, 2011; Dye et al., 2014).
Nonorganizational research can also be beneficial in helping us understand how coalitions connect with outside interests, specifically in regard to the structure and boundaries of coalitions. For example, game theoretical models suggest that potential members can become interested in bargains due to external influences, and they may, in turn, affect others who are not a party to the conflict (Sandler, 2003). Thus, external influences can manifest in coalition formation or partitioning. Similarly, the evidence in political science suggests that internal participants who share a number of direct and indirect connections with outsiders are unable to insulate themselves from external influence (Haas, 1992). This is more likely when there are shared interests between participants and outsiders, even if the influence is not a reflection of formal consent (Pion-Berlin, 2004). Does this imply that coalition members with strong external social networks act differently than those with fewer connections? One way to understand this is to capitalize on differences in the role of external coalitions in RDT and the PFM, with the former offering insights into the independent effect of external stakeholders on coalition preferences and the latter revealing how external concerns are disseminated through internal members.
Another possible benefit from nonorganizational research is in understanding influence processes, particularly because in areas such as game theory, side payments are easy to observe. They can allow us to develop models of the bargaining process that help explain questions such as: How often are bargains renegotiated? To what extent do individuals on the opposite sides of a negotiation compromise due to factors other than side payments? Some answers are already available from the discussion of trust in game theory. Since greater trust between actors makes them more responsive to each other’s needs (McCabe, Rigdon, & Smith, 2003), members with long-term interactions cannot be completely indifferent to others’ losses (Stevenson et al., 1985). This suggests the benefit of examining how trust affects bargaining between coalitions.

Conclusion

A foundational element in the BTF (Cyert et al., 1956, 1958; Cyert & March, 1963; March, 1962), coalitions have become a valuable construct in the management literature. In their preface to the second edition of A Behavioral Theory of the Firm, Cyert and March (1992: xi) wrote that “since 1963, conventions of theoretical discourse about organizational decision making have changed, and a number of the ideas discussed in the book have become part of received doctrine. In particular, a perspective that sees firms as coalitions of multiple, conflicting interests. . . .” Despite this progress, the study of organizational coalitions has suffered from the very phenomenon it is expected to explain: conflicting perspectives, which has led to a fragmented view of its actors, structures, and processes (Hu et al., 2017; Murnighan & Brass, 1991).
Taking stock of almost six decades of research on organizational coalitions, our review offers a synthesis of overlaps and contrasts in the role of coalitions as a vehicle for collective political influence. The review revealed significant agreement around the definition of coalitions’ actors, their structural positions, and the perpetual nature of conflict and compromises. The literature is inconsistent, however, with regard to actors’ interactions and motivations, the structural manifestation of coalitions in organizations, and the basis and outcomes of coalitions’ conflicts and negotiations. The contrasts correspond to six different conceptualizations: the BTF coalition, the RDT coalition, the IT coalition, the UET coalition, the PFM coalition, and the OS coalition. Building on these contrasts, we developed an organizing framework that advances a dynamic model of organizational coalitions. The model presents organizational coalitions as a multifaceted construct with the flexibility to respond to diverse environmental and organizational challenges.
Our review advances the discussion of organizational coalitions in three specific ways. First, we offer a systematic understanding of the different ways in which collective political influence operates in organizations. It clarifies that coalitions are not just vehicles for political influence, they represent deeper theoretical conceptualizations that embody identifying assumptions about organizational actors, their relationships with an organization’s internal and external environment, and the bargaining processes that shape decision-making. A change in the conceptualization of coalitions changes the way we view organizational decision-making, the corresponding political maneuvers, and in turn, the goals that coalitions are expected to achieve.
Second, we show that while the divergence between conceptualizations is critical when the discussion is situated within the boundaries of a single theory, relaxing those boundaries reveals theoretical pathways that facilitate the coalition from one stream of research to be shaped into another stream’s conceptualization. This enables more realistic models of the organization’s political environment that neither place constraints on theoretical development when accommodating empirical evidence nor are constrained by theoretical boundaries. In turn, the potential for a representative understanding allows organizational members to develop an effective understanding of the political features in their environment and, possibly, to more adequately respond to how they influence and are influenced by others.
Finally, because the concept of coalitions is a foundational feature of the various substreams of the BTF, our organizational framework brings us closer to a general theory of organizational coalitions. Such a theory can integrate the advancements that have occurred in the various substreams of the BTF and offer a more complete understanding of the behavioral influences that affect organizational choices. We believe this review helps set the stage for greater scholarship in this area.

Differences Between Coalitions, Alliances, and Partnerships

During our review, we came across several accounts that treated coalitions as a partnership or an alliance. For example, Eisenhardt and Bourgeois (1988: 760) observed that “[o]ver time, a pattern of stable alliances emerges in politically active teams” and Zhang (2006: 286) argued that “[w]hen the CEO is powerful, the COO/president tends to partner with the CEO rather than to contest the CEO.” To avoid ambiguity, we address below some of the small but critical differences between coalitions, alliances, and partnerships.
The goal of an alliance is the pursuit of shared interests over a specific period and, in the case of a partnership, identical interests over a longer horizon. In contrast, a coalition is shaped around interests that overlap with regards to the outcomes but less so in terms of the underlying motivations (Dyson, 1976). These agreements are between friends in partnerships and acquaintances in alliances but often include adversaries in coalitions. They turn long-term agreements that are common to partnerships and short-term arrangements pursued in alliances into ongoing deliberations in coalitions. Divergent self-interests limit the extent to which coalition members are willing to communicate with each other or share information and resources. This perpetuates internal conflict, which, in an alliance, is passive when it exists and, in a partnership, judiciously resolved (Fedder, 1968; Gamson, 1961; Mesterton-Gibbons, Gavrilets, Gravner, & Akcay, 2011). The potential for conflict also makes coalitions ineffective in the face of external incentives, deployed frequently by opposing groups to turn the members against each other (Kelley, 1976). The effectiveness and therefore the threat of incentives is significantly lower for an alliance and even less so for a partnership because of the shared and identical interests (Pan & Pan, 2006; Stevenson et al., 1985). Yet due to their loose internal coordination whereby participants are often united in perception but divided in action, coalitions are more difficult for an opposing group to combat. This makes coalitions effective political tactics for annoying, confusing, and exhausting the opposition. In contrast, alliances are driven toward clarity and capacity for operational choices and partnerships toward more strategic goals (Riker, 1962; Thibaut & Kelley, 1959).
